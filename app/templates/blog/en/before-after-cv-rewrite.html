A generic CV and an ATS-optimised one can describe the same person with the same experience — and produce completely different results. The difference isn't embellishment or exaggeration. It's structure, language, and specificity. This is what that looks like in practice — for someone whose career has nothing to do with tech.

Advice about CVs tends to stay abstract. "Use achievement-focused language." "Include keywords from the job description." "Avoid generic summaries." All correct. All easy to nod at and then ignore when you're staring at your own document.

This post walks through a real rewrite, decision by decision. Same candidate, same experience, different CV. The candidate is in People & Culture — because the same principles apply whether you work in HR, healthcare, education, finance, or construction. The ATS doesn't care what industry you're in.

Meet the original CV

The candidate is Sarah — a People & Culture professional with six years of experience across talent acquisition, employee relations, and learning & development. She's applying for a People & Culture Manager role at a mid-size healthcare organisation. The job description emphasises talent strategy, employee engagement, HRIS implementation, ER case management, and workforce planning.

Her CV is a two-column PDF she built in Canva two years ago and has updated in place ever since. The file is named CV-FINAL.pdf. The left sidebar contains her contact details, a visual skills list with rating bars, and her education. The right column has her work history. She's used the same professional summary since she took her first generalist HR role.

On screen, it looks polished. In an ATS, it's a parsing nightmare.

What's wrong with it — and why

Problem 1: The summary hasn't been written for this role.
The current summary reads: "Motivated HR professional with experience across the employee lifecycle, committed to supporting organisational culture and people initiatives."
This describes a category of professionals, not a person. It contains no specifics about Sarah's actual strengths, no indication of seniority, and none of the language the job description uses. An ATS scanning for "talent acquisition," "ER case management," or "Workday HRIS" will find no matches here at all.

Problem 2: Duties, not achievements.
Her experience bullets read like a position description, not a record of impact. Examples: "Managed recruitment processes for open roles." "Coordinated onboarding for new employees." "Supported employee relations cases as required." These say what she was responsible for, not what she accomplished. There are no numbers, no outcomes, no context for scale — a hiring manager can't tell whether she handled three hires a year or forty-five.

Problem 3: Missing keywords.
The job description uses "talent acquisition," "employee engagement," "Workday HRIS," "ER case management," and "workforce planning" prominently. None of these phrases appear in Sarah's CV, even though she does all of them. The ATS screening for these terms will score her poorly — not because she lacks the skills, but because she used different language to describe them.

Problem 4: Formatting risk.
The Canva-generated two-column PDF will parse badly in most ATS systems. The visual skill bars in the sidebar will be invisible to the parser. The contact details in the left column may be extracted in the wrong order or skipped entirely. The text the ATS extracts will be garbled — this isn't a tech problem specific to IT professionals, it affects every CV built in a design tool.

Problem 5: Leadership scope not visible.
Sarah informally supervised two HR coordinators and ran a full 40-person graduate intake program from end to end. Her current title is "People & Culture Advisor." The Manager role she's applying for is one level up, and her CV does nothing to bridge that gap — the supervision and program ownership are simply never mentioned. The gap looks bigger than it is.

The rewrite, step by step

New summary

Before: "Motivated HR professional with experience across the employee lifecycle, committed to supporting organisational culture and people initiatives."

After: "People & Culture professional with six years of experience in talent acquisition, employee relations, and workforce planning across healthcare and professional services. Track record in full-cycle recruitment, ER case management, and HRIS administration (Workday). Informally led a team of two HR coordinators and managed a 40-person graduate intake program end-to-end. Seeking a P&C Manager role to formalise that leadership while deepening strategic HR work."

The rewrite does four things: names her specialisation and level, includes the keywords the posting uses, quantifies her scope, and bridges the gap between her current title and the role she's targeting. It also drops "committed to supporting" entirely — vague intent language adds nothing.

Achievement bullet rewrites

Before: "Managed recruitment processes for open roles."
After: "Led end-to-end talent acquisition for 45+ roles annually across three business units, reducing average time-to-fill from 52 to 34 days by introducing structured interview panel briefings and standardised candidate feedback loops."

Before: "Coordinated onboarding for new employees."
After: "Redesigned the new employee onboarding program for a 300-person organisation, reducing 90-day voluntary turnover from 18% to 9% over two years."

Before: "Supported employee relations cases as required."
After: "Managed 30+ ER cases annually — including performance management, workplace investigations, and EBA interpretation — with no adverse findings across two consecutive audit cycles."

Each rewrite follows the same principle: replace the duty with the outcome, add the scale that makes it credible, and use the same language the employer is using to search for it.

Keyword additions

The job description's core terms — talent acquisition, employee engagement, Workday HRIS, ER case management, workforce planning, onboarding — are now present in the summary and woven naturally into the experience bullets. They weren't invented. They were already in Sarah's history. The rewrite just uses the employer's vocabulary to describe them.

Formatting fix

The Canva PDF is replaced with a clean single-column document. Contact details move to the top of the main body. The visual skill bars are replaced with a plain-text skills section: "HRIS: Workday (advanced), BambooHR (intermediate) | ER & Compliance: Fair Work Act, EBA interpretation, workplace investigations | Talent Acquisition: full-cycle recruitment, LinkedIn Recruiter, structured interviewing." Parseable, scannable, specific.

The final version — what changed and why it works better

The final CV describes the same person with the same career history. Nothing has been invented. The employer is the same. The tenure is the same. The title is unchanged.

What changed: the summary positions Sarah for Manager-level work instead of advisor-level work. The bullets show outcomes and scale instead of job duties. The key terms from the job description appear naturally throughout the document. The formatting is single-column and ATS-safe. The most important leadership work — the supervision, the grad program — is now visible, where before it was invisible because it was never stated.

The result is a CV that accurately represents Sarah's capabilities, instead of underselling them — and that will pass ATS screening where the original would likely have been filtered before a human ever read it.

Key takeaways

  • Your CV is a translation document. You're translating your experience into the language the employer is using in their job description. The skills are the same. The vocabulary matters — and this is true whether you're in HR, nursing, teaching, finance, or engineering.
  • Numbers unlock credibility. You don't need impressive numbers — you need honest, specific ones. "Reduced turnover from 18% to 9%" is more convincing than "improved retention outcomes."
  • Formatting is infrastructure. A CV that doesn't parse is a CV that doesn't get read. Structure your document for the parser first, the human reader second — and it will work for both.
  • Your best material is often invisible because you never stated it. Informal supervision, program ownership, budget management — these often happen without the title. If you did the work, put it on the page.
